How Mike D'Antoni Changed The NBA



The Houston Rockets own the NBA's best record and are having one of the best offensive seasons in history. With head coach Mike D'Antoni at the helm, the Rockets look a lot like his Phoenix Suns who famously played an up-tempo brand of basketball in the mid-2000s. As a Western Conference Final against the Golden State Warriors becomes a real possibility, how did this offensive guru come to play catch up to his own revolutionary methods that changed the league more than a decade before?


By Daniel Levitt


February 18, 2018


Golden State is often credited with being the pioneers of today's pace-and-space style of NBA, which has seen more and more teams pushing their pace and shooting more three pointers. But earlier signs of playing up-tempo can be traced back to D'Antoni's first stint as an NBA head coach with Denver almost 20 years ago. D'Antoni took over the Nuggets for the lockout-shortened 1999 season and finished second in three-point attempts, up from 20th the previous season. But a disappointing 14-36 record and only four months of the regular season wasn't enough time to see his new system work and D'Antoni was fired in September of that year.



Seven Seconds Or Less


D'Antoni returned to Italy to coach Benetton Treviso for the 2002 season and had success with his 'run-and-gun' system, which was enough for Bryan Colangelo, then the general manager of the Phoenix Suns, to buy into D'Antoni's philosophy and give him another shot in the NBA. And it worked.


In his first two full seasons in Phoenix, D'Antoni took the Suns to back-to-back Western Conference Finals, each time leading the league in pace of play -- number of possessions per 48 minutes -- and number of three point attempts. With dynamic point guard Steve Nash, D'Antoni finally had the floor general that he needed to lead his team. They would never get over the penultimate hurdle, but the relentless 'Seven Seconds Or Less' Suns sent shockwaves through the league that are still being felt today, with almost every team playing faster than they've ever played.

Pace And Space


It wasn't just the ferocious pace at which the Suns played that would be revolutionary. Accompanied on the court by at least two three-point specialists at all times, Nash would have a field day attracting multiple defenders by driving to the hoop and then passing out to the likes of Leandro Barbosa and Raja Bell, who would knock down the open three.


When Colangelo resigned as the Suns' GM mid-season in 2006, it signaled the end of an era. Steve Kerr eventually filled the role, but a series of dubious trades disrupted D'Antoni's flowing system. The most notable came on February 6, 2008.


With the team in first place in the West and owners of a 34-15 record, Kerr traded the 29-year-old Shawn Marion for a 35-year-old Shaquille O'Neal. Kerr later admitted that it was a mistake to substitute the bustling Marion — who played a key cog in the D'Antoni system — for an aging and slower O'Neal. And for good reason. The Suns finished that year as the sixth seed and exited the playoffs in the first round. Two weeks later, D'Antoni left Phoenix to become the head coach of the New York Knicks.



Thank Goodness For Golden State


Three-and-a-half disappointing seasons with the Knicks, followed by an anticlimactic couple of years with the Lakers, left D'Antoni out of the league and mulling his next options at home in West Virginia. That was, until he saw the Warriors steamroll their way to an NBA championship in 2015, playing a brand of the 'Seven Seconds Or Less' Suns, but on steroids. Finally, D'Antoni felt vindicated that his methods had been enough to win it all.


Now in Houston, D'Antoni again has a transcendent guard in James Harden that can push the pace and find open teammates to shoot the three. After witnessing the Warriors become arguably the greatest team ever built from the foundation of shooting threes, it's ironic to see the Rockets 'out three' the Warriors. After shattering the record last season for most three-point attempts with 40, Houston is on pace to break it yet again, this time by shooting more than 42 a game.

Three Is More Than Two


The effects of D'Antoni's vision two decades ago have been emphatically felt throughout the league, and there might be more to come. As the rest of the league catches up with the Rockets and Warriors, the league as a whole is only going to become even more efficient. Take Toronto for example, who ranked 23rd in pace last season and changed their offense this year to mimic where the rest of the league was headed. The payoff? The Raptors are currently enjoying the 28th best offensive efficiency rating since the ABA-NBA merger in 1976.


As for this year's Rockets and Warriors, they're in a category all of their own: Houston currently has the fourth-best combined offensive rating and effective field goal percentage — which takes into account the balance of twos and threes taken — in a single season in history. And who's in front of them? Every Golden State team over the past three seasons.
