US companies cut ties to NRA after school shooting

Pressure from customers and campaigners forces corporate America to act

Daniel Levitt in Florida
FEBRUARY 18, 2018

Delta Air Lines, United Airlines and MetLife were among several prominent U.S. companies to cut ties with the National Rifle Association last week, after 17 people were shot and killed at Marjory Stoneman Douglas High School in Parkland, Florida. The latest episode in America's bloody history of guns injected another layer into an already complicated issue and forced corporate America to pick a side.

The morality of businesses' associations with the NRA was thrust into the public sphere in 2012, when 27 people — 20 of whom were children between the ages of six and seven — were gunned down at Sandy Hook Elementary School in Newtown, Connecticut. Pressure continued to percolate for years, hitting the headlines every time another deadly incident occurred. It appears that the Parkland shooting — already the 34th of its kind in the U.S. in 2018 — was enough to finally infiltrate the arteries of some of the world's largest companies.

Unfortunately the first two months of this year haven't been an anomaly. Last year, mass shootings — defined as all shooting incidents in which four or more people are killed or injured by gunfire — happened on 200 days, or almost 60 percent of the calendar year.

Outrage aimed at NRA-funded politicians followed by little to no action has become a routine in the aftermath of shootings in recent years. The NRA has been too big and too powerful for gun control campaigners to make any headway for change. But that's where this latest chapter is different from the rest.

In a Quinnipiac University poll conducted last week, support for a number of gun control measures reached an all-time high from American voters. The Parkland shooting is not the first to target school children, nor is it the deadliest. But as public opinion on gun control moves into new territory, the status quo has begun to crack.
